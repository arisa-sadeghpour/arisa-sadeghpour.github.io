About

I'm a PhD candidate in Statistics at UC Berkeley, where I develop causal inference methods for data that doesn't fit the textbook case — settings with network structure, spatial dependence, or shifting estimands, motivated primarily by questions in the social sciences.

My recent work looks at how residualization and outcome modeling can produce shorter, still-valid confidence intervals under design-based inference, and at what changes when the resampling process itself varies. Alongside this, I've worked on applied problems spanning voting behavior, evictions, and disease modeling, and spent a summer at Stanford's RegLab thinking about how to evaluate deployed AI systems with causal tools.

Before Berkeley, I studied Computational and Applied Mathematics at Rice University, where a civic leadership certificate pulled me toward using statistics for questions that affect real communities — a thread that's stayed central to my research since.
