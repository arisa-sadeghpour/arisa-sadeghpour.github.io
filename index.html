I work on causal inference methods for settings with complex data — designing estimators that stay valid when the data is messy, structured, or entangled, with an eye toward applications in the social sciences.

About

I'm a PhD candidate in Statistics at UC Berkeley, where I develop causal inference methods for data that doesn't fit the textbook case — settings with network structure, spatial dependence, or shifting estimands. My recent work looks at how residualization and outcome modeling can produce shorter, still-valid confidence intervals under design-based inference, alongside applied projects spanning voting behavior, evictions, and disease modeling, and a summer at Stanford's RegLab evaluating deployed AI systems with causal tools. Before Berkeley, I studied Computational and Applied Mathematics at Rice University, where a civic leadership certificate pulled me toward using statistics for questions that affect real communities — a thread that's stayed central to my research since.
